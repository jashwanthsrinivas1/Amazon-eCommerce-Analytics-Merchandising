# Project 02 — Interactive AC Buying Guide

**Type:** Customer-Facing Product Discovery Tool — End-to-End Ownership
**Category:** Air Conditioners (AC)
**Marketplace:** Amazon.in
**Role:** Senior Site Merchandiser (problem owner, solution designer, merchandising lead)
**Period:** August 2022 – September 2024

---

## Business Problem

The Air Conditioner category on Amazon India offers hundreds of products across multiple brands, tonnage capacities, energy ratings, technologies, and price points. For a first-time or infrequent buyer, this variety creates significant decision paralysis.

**The challenge:** A customer arriving on the AC category page knows what they want in real-world terms — "I have a medium-sized bedroom" or "I want something that turns off automatically" — but they do not know how those needs translate into product specifications. They don't think in terms of "1.5-ton 5-star split inverter AC." They think in terms of their room, their budget, and the features that matter to them.

This gap between how customers think and how products are listed was causing:
- High bounce rates on the AC category page
- Customers leaving to research on external sites before returning to purchase
- Missed opportunities to guide customers toward the right product segment and price tier

**The opportunity:** Build an interactive tool that bridges this gap — one that asks customers simple questions about their real-world situation and translates their answers into the right product recommendation.

---

## Solution: The Interactive AC Buying Guide

The AC Buying Guide was a structured, interactive recommendation tool embedded on Amazon.in's Air Conditioner category page. Unlike a static filter panel, it was designed as a guided decision flow — customers answered a series of simple questions and the tool surfaced a curated set of matching products.

### How It Worked

The tool was built around a branching logic framework. Each customer input narrowed the product recommendation set:

**Step 1 — Room Size**

The customer selects their room size. The tool maps room size to the correct AC capacity:

| Room Size | Recommended Capacity |
|---|---|
| Up to 100 sq ft | Less than 1 Ton |
| 100 to 150 sq ft | 1 Ton to 1.5 Ton |
| 150 to 200 sq ft | 1.5 Ton |
| 200 sq ft and above | 2 Ton and above |

**Step 2 — Feature Preference**

The customer selects which features matter most. Each feature maps to a specific product subset:

| Customer Preference | Products Shown |
|---|---|
| Auto Turn Off | ACs with auto cut-off and timer functionality |
| Energy Saving | 5-star rated ACs, inverter technology |
| Smart / Wi-Fi Control | Smart ACs controllable via app |
| Hot & Cold | Year-round ACs with heating capability |
| Convertible Cooling | Convertible ACs (5-in-1, 6-in-1 modes) |
| Low Noise | ACs rated for quiet operation |

**Step 3 — Budget Range**

The customer selects a price band. The tool filters within the already-narrowed capacity and feature set to show products within budget.

**Output:** A curated grid of 3–6 products matching all three inputs — room size, feature preference, and budget — with callout copy explaining why each product fits the customer's stated needs.

## My End-to-End Involvement

### 1. Problem Definition
- Identified the customer drop-off problem through slot-level analytics and category page data
- Documented the problem statement: customers arriving with real-world needs but no structured path to match those needs to products
- Built the business case for an interactive solution vs. a static filter improvement

### 2. Solution Design
- Designed the full logic framework — the room size to capacity mapping, the feature to product subset mapping, and the budget filtering layer
- Created the decision tree structure defining how each customer input combination maps to a product output
- Wrote the customer-facing copy for each section — callout headlines, feature descriptions, and recommendation labels
- Documented the requirements specification shared with the development team

### 3. Development Coordination
- Worked directly with the development team to translate the logic framework into a functional interactive tool
- Defined the slot structure — how many products to show per combination, which fields to display (product name, key feature, starting price), and how to handle edge cases
- Reviewed builds and provided feedback on UX flow and product mapping accuracy
- Iterated on the logic as new products launched or deal pricing changed

### 4. Merchandising the Site
- Managed the live deployment of the Buying Guide on the AC category page
- Curated and updated ASIN selections within each recommendation bucket as inventory and deals changed
- Maintained the product mapping document ensuring each combination pointed to the best-value products available

### 5. Promotion
- Coordinated placement of the Buying Guide within the AC category page slot structure at high-visibility positions
- Aligned with the campaign team to feature Buying Guide products within concurrent coupon and affordability campaigns
- Supported push notification campaigns pointing customers to Buying Guide-featured products

## Outcome

- **25% improvement in product selection efficiency** — customers reached a purchase decision faster when guided by the tool vs. browsing unassisted
- Reduced customer navigation depth — fewer clicks required to find a relevant product
- Improved relevance of product recommendations surfaced to customers, particularly for first-time AC buyers unfamiliar with technical specifications
- Created a reusable framework that could be extended to other Large Appliances categories

---

## What Made This Project Different

Most merchandising work is reactive — you get a product, you place it on a page. This project was different because it required:

- **Analytical thinking:** Understanding the gap between customer intent and product taxonomy
- **Systems thinking:** Designing a logic tree that handles dozens of input combinations consistently
- **Product thinking:** Defining the user experience from the customer's perspective, not the catalog's perspective
- **Technical collaboration:** Working with a development team to implement interactive logic, not just static content
- **Operational ownership:** Keeping the tool current as products, prices, and deals changed over time
